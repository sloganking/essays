Why Good People Cause the Most Harm

By Logan King

When I was a kid, my father hit me for doing my homework too slowly.

He wasn't a monster. He was afraid. He looked at his son and saw a boy who would crumble when the world got hard — and the world does get hard, he knew that firsthand — so he tried to toughen me up the only way he understood. His intentions were love. His methods were violence. And the outcome was the opposite of everything he wanted.

That gap — between what he meant and what he made — is the most important thing I know. Because it isn't just my father. It's the engine behind most of the harm human beings cause each other. And almost no one can see it while they're doing it.

We're looking for the wrong thing

We're taught that harm comes from bad people. Predators. Villains. People who want to hurt us. So we scan for them. We build our entire sense of safety around spotting the wolf before it reaches us.

But the person about to do you the most damage usually isn't a villain. It's someone certain they're protecting something — a parent, a partner, a community, a cause. Someone whose love or fear has hardened into certainty. And certainty doesn't check itself.

We have a word for someone who hurts you for their own gain. We call them a predator, and everyone knows what it means. We don't have a word for someone who causes equal or greater harm while genuinely believing they're saving people. So we call them a hero. Or — if we happen to be the one they're hurting — we call them an asshole, and no one believes us, because they so obviously look like they're doing good.

The missing word hides the bigger danger. Our language pre-frames us to watch for the selfish predator and walk right past the righteous one doing ten times the damage.

"Of all tyrannies, a tyranny sincerely exercised for the good of its victims may be the most oppressive… those who torment us for our own good will torment us without end, for they do so with the approval of their own conscience."

— C.S. Lewis

The Inquisitors weren't cynics. They believed they were saving souls from eternal fire. That belief is exactly what let them torture with a clean conscience. A selfish predator is one person doing harm. Someone certain they're righteous can move an entire room — and never once feel the weight of what they've done, because from the inside it feels like rescue.

You become the thing you fear

Here's the part that should stop you: the people most afraid of predators are the ones most likely to become one.

Fear narrows what you can see. Walk into a room scanning for danger and you will find it — every ambiguous touch becomes suspicious, every awkward moment becomes evidence, every mistake becomes confirmation. Not because the threats are real. Because that's what you're looking for, and the human mind is very good at finding what it's looking for.

So someone makes an honest, recoverable mistake. A person primed for danger reads it as an attack and sounds the alarm. The whole room's attention flips from "friends" to "threats," and now everyone is finding proof of a predator who was never there. Someone who fawns and apologizes out of fear looks like they're confessing. The crowd doesn't weigh evidence — it reads intensity. If they're that upset, something terrible must have happened.

And the person at the center takes the real, permanent damage — the destroyed reputation, the lost community — in response to something that warranted a conversation. The fear didn't prevent harm. The fear was the harm.

If you cry victim loud enough, you become the perpetrator.

I know this machine from the inside. I've been the one whose fear rewrote reality — certain I'd been wronged when the other person did exactly what they said they would. And I've been the one a room turned on, watched myself get cast as the monster while the people who could see clearly stayed quiet, because speaking up against a certain crowd means becoming its next target.

Where I learned to see it

I didn't learn this from a book. I learned it in the hardest room I could have walked into.

For years I lived in sex-positive spaces — play parties, tantra workshops, intimacy retreats. People go there to heal, and most of them are doing real, brave work. But it is also the single place our culture sees least clearly. It's where the most fear lives, where the word "predator" gets thrown the fastest, and where the gap between what someone meant and what someone felt is the widest. When a person is naked and vulnerable and a mistake happens, there's nowhere to hide — not for the person who made it, and not for the one whose reaction reveals more about their story than about what actually occurred.

So I spent years learning to see clearly in exactly that room — building a way to tell a predator from a scared person, a real wound from a story, a mistake from malice. A framework that can survive that room can survive any room. That's why I built it there on purpose.

And once I could spot that machine — good people, certain and afraid, doing harm they can't see — I started seeing it everywhere. The same one runs behind racism — good people certain of beliefs that aren't true. Behind war — good people on both sides, each certain the other is evil, each willing to destroy to protect what they love. Behind the family that shatters because everyone is sure they're the one who was wronged. Good people. Harmful beliefs. Devastating outcomes. Not despite the love — because of the certainty that rides in with it.

The part you can't see

Here's the catch, and it's the whole game: you can't spot your own version of this.

Belief-blindness doesn't feel like blindness. It feels like being right. It feels like seeing clearly. The Inquisitor felt exactly as certain as you feel right now. The mother defending her child, the partner protecting the relationship, the person who knows — really knows — what happened in that room: every one of them is looking through a lens they can't see, and calling it reality.

If you read all of this and your first thought is how clearly you can see this in the people around you — that's the check engine light. Learning the name of the pattern doesn't lift it off you. The beliefs running most of your life are the ones that don't feel like beliefs at all. They feel like the way things are.

And here's the hard part: the patterns shaping your relationships, your reactions, your life are the hardest for you to see — for the same reason you can't see your own eyes. You can understand every word of this and still miss the specific ones running you.

Keep going

I wrote a book about all of it — how to see the pattern in others and in yourself before it costs you something you can't get back. It uses the hardest arena I know as the laboratory, and everything in it works far beyond that arena: your relationships, your team, your family, the next room you walk into where someone is certain and afraid.
